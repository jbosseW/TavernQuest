# PART I: THE HOLLOWED

## The Hunger That Wears a Face

---

They are called by many names across the races of the Known World, but the thing itself has no name. Names are for creatures that exist within the order of creation. What makes a Hollowed is older than order. Older than the gods. Older than the word "old."

A Hollowed begins as a person. Any person. Any race.

The transformation requires a single act: consuming one's own kind. Not merely eating flesh — animals do that and remain animals. This is the difference between appetite and the loss of self. When a sentient being eats the flesh of another sentient being of its own race, something fundamental tears. A membrane between what *is* and what *was before* rips open, and through that wound, something enters.

Not a demon. Not a spirit, precisely. The scholars who have survived studying the phenomenon describe it as an echo of primordial hunger, a fragment of the appetite that existed before the world was given shape. Before the gods pushed back the darkness. Before the First Word was spoken.

Some things remember a time before creation. And some of those things are always hungry.

The consumed flesh acts as a key. The act of cannibalism, the consumption of self by self, the reduction of personhood to meat, unmakes the barrier between existence and the nothing that preceded it. In that moment of unmaking, the Hunger enters.

It does not possess. Possession implies a separate entity displacing the original.

The Hunger does not replace the person.

**It fills what was already hollow.**

---

### The Connection to Yr'shalos

Those few scholars who have studied both the Vel'sharath Heresy and the phenomenon of the Hollowed have noted a resonance between them. The Void-That-Hungers, Yr'shalos, the Absence Made Flesh, the entity the Hollow Circle nearly summoned into the world five hundred years ago, is described in every surviving text as hunger given form. Not a being that hungers. *Hunger itself.*

The Hollowed are not servants of Yr'shalos. Not cultists or worshipers.

They are symptoms.

When the Last Wardens created the Seal of Remembrance to hold Yr'shalos at bay, they bound the entity behind a metaphysical barrier maintained by the world's memory of itself. But seals leak. Walls crack. And through those cracks, fragments of the primordial appetite seep into reality like groundwater through limestone.

These fragments have no intelligence, no will, no agenda. They are simply *hunger*... raw, unthinking, infinite. They drift through the spaces between things, waiting for the moment a sentient being tears a hole in the fabric of existence.

The act of cannibalism reduces a person, a consciousness, a soul, a participant in the memory that holds back the Void, to nothing more than calories. It says: *you were never anything more than meat.* And in that moment, the Hunger recognizes a kindred impulse.

It enters. It settles. It *stays.*

Each Hollowed is a tiny breach, a pinhole in the barrier between existence and oblivion. Most scholars who reach this conclusion do not survive to publish their findings. Not because anything kills them. Because they stop wanting to exist.

The Luminary Inquest knows none of this. Or if they do, they will never admit it.

---

### How They Are Made

The first act is always deliberate. No one becomes Hollowed by accident. The flesh must be consumed knowingly; the eater must understand what they are eating. A soldier fed mystery rations during a siege does not turn. A starving child tricked into eating does not turn.

The eater must look at the flesh and know: *this was a person. This was like me.* And then choose to eat anyway.

The reasons vary:

- **Desperation.** Famine. Siege. Shipwreck. The circumstances where survival overrides every other consideration. These are the most tragic cases: people who crossed a line they never imagined crossing, and found that the line was a door.
- **Madness.** Some minds simply break. The act follows the fracture, not the other way around.
- **Ritual.** In the deep places of the world, in forgotten cults and isolated communities, there are those who consume flesh deliberately, seeking power. They find it. They find more than they wanted.
- **Compulsion.** A Hollowed that has fed can, in rare cases, induce the urge in others through proximity. This is how clusters form. One Hollowed in a village can create three more within a year if left unchecked.

The transformation is not instant.

After the first feeding, the person feels... nothing. Perhaps a moment of nausea. Perhaps not even that. They return to their life. They go to work. They smile at their neighbors. They sleep beside their spouse.

But the Hunger is already inside, curled in the cavity behind the ribs like a worm in an apple. It is patient. It does not rush.

**First week:** Restlessness. Difficulty sleeping. Food tastes wrong... not bad, simply *insufficient.* As if every meal is missing an ingredient the body cannot name.

**First month:** The cravings begin. Not for flesh specifically, not yet. For *satiation.* For the feeling of being full. The Hollowed eats more, but the feeling of fullness never comes. Friends notice increased appetite. The Hollowed jokes about a growth spurt. Everyone laughs.

**First three months:** The cravings sharpen. Meat becomes the only thing that temporarily dulls the ache. The Hollowed begins eating their steaks rarer. Their sausages less cooked. They develop a taste for organ meats, for marrow, for the parts of the animal that other diners leave on the plate. No one comments. People's tastes change.

**First six months:** The first relapse. The Hunger has grown loud enough to drown out conscience, loud enough to turn a passing stranger into an opportunity. The second feeding is easier than the first. Afterward, the Hollowed feels full for the first time in months. The relief is so profound it borders on ecstasy.

**After the second feeding:** The cycle accelerates. Each feeding brings shorter periods of satiation. The Hunger grows faster than it is fed. The intervals between kills compress: six months, three months, one month, two weeks.

**The fully Hollowed:** A creature that exists in a state of permanent, screaming starvation. It can eat until its stomach ruptures and feel nothing. It can consume an entire person and be hungry again before the blood dries. The Hunger has become everything it is. It is a mouth pretending to be a person.

And yet, and this is what makes them truly terrifying, **they look completely normal.**

---

### The Mask

A Hollowed does not change in appearance. Not at rest. Not in its daily life. It goes to work. It buys bread at the market. It complains about the weather and the tax collector. It kisses its children goodnight.

The transformation is internal and invisible.

The person is still there. The original personality is not consumed or displaced. It *coexists* with the Hunger, the way a person coexists with a tapeworm, aware of its presence on some deep, animal level, but unable to remove it. Unable to even articulate what has changed.

Most Hollowed do not think of themselves as monsters. They compartmentalize. The feeding is something that happens at night, in the dark, in the spaces between their real life. The disappearances in the neighborhood are unrelated. The stains on their clothes are from working late. The dreams, the terrible, beautiful dreams of an endless feast in a darkness that has no end, are just dreams.

They maintain relationships. They hold jobs. Some are respected members of their communities. A Hollowed can serve on a town council, lead a prayer circle, teach children their letters. The mask is not a disguise the Hunger wears. The mask *is* the person. A person who has simply added one more unthinkable behavior to a life otherwise defined by normalcy.

No mark. No visible corruption. No glowing eyes or infernal brands. The Luminary Inquest's diagnostic tools, designed to detect demonic influence, arcane contamination, and vampiric infection, return negative on a Hollowed. The Hunger predates demons, arcana, and undeath; it is older than any category the Inquest has a name for.

The clues are subtle. Almost invisible. And they require the observer to be looking for something that most people refuse to believe exists.

---

### Identifying the Hollowed: Clues Before the Confrontation

The following signs have been documented across multiple cases, compiled from sources that the Luminary Inquest classifies as "unreliable folk superstition":

**Behavioral Signs:**
- **Missing persons** in the Hollowed's vicinity. Not dramatic disappearances, but the kind of quiet vanishings that get filed as "probably left town" or "likely fell in the river."
- **Increased appetite** paired with **weight loss.** The Hollowed eats constantly but grows gaunt. The body is burning through itself, unable to extract nutrition from anything but the one food it truly craves.
- **Changed relationship with food.** Avoiding communal meals. Eating alone. A sudden disinterest in food that once brought pleasure, paired with an obsessive interest in meat.
- **Isolation.** Gradual withdrawal from social life. Not dramatic, but subtle. Shorter conversations. Fewer visits. A spouse who mentions, almost as an afterthought, that their partner "hasn't been quite right" for months.
- **Unnatural calm.** Between feedings, the Hollowed enters a state of eerie composure. Nothing bothers them. Nothing excites them. They have the patience of the deeply satisfied or the deeply broken.

**Environmental Signs:**
- **Animals react.** Dogs growl at the Hollowed for no reason. Horses refuse to let them approach. Birds fall silent when they pass. Cats stare, not with fear, but with recognition. Beast folk oral traditions suggest that cats, whether sapient or common, can perceive the Hunger's presence. They will sit and watch a Hollowed with unblinking attention, tracking their movements with the intensity of a predator watching prey... or prey watching a predator.
- **Cold spots.** The area immediately around a Hollowed is subtly colder than its surroundings. Not enough to see breath, but enough to notice on a warm day. The Hunger consumes more than flesh: warmth, presence, the ambient energy of being alive. People standing near a Hollowed step away without knowing why.
- **The smell.** Faint. Irregular. A hint of rot that comes and goes. Not the smell of a corpse, but the smell of a cavity. An empty space where something should be. Perfumers and alchemists sometimes notice it when others cannot.
- **Spoilage.** Food left near a Hollowed spoils faster than it should. Milk curdles. Bread molds overnight. Fruit darkens.

**Supernatural Signs (for those with the ability to perceive them):**
- **Sanctioned mages** may detect a "null zone" around the Hollowed, not the presence of something, but the *absence* of magical background radiation. The world is slightly less *real* in their immediate vicinity.
- **Helios priests** report their blessings feeling "hollow" or "thin" near a Hollowed, as if the divine energy is being dispersed before it can take effect.
- **The Veil** (in Shadow Fen border regions) behaves erratically around the Hollowed, sometimes strengthening, sometimes weakening, as if uncertain whether to classify them as inside or outside reality.

### The True Form

A Hollowed maintains its mask through the routine of daily life. The transformation into the True Form is triggered by **direct confrontation**: when the Hollowed is accused, cornered, or attacked with lethal intent. When the mask is no longer sustainable, the Hunger stops pretending.

**The transformation takes seconds.**

The body elongates. Limbs stretch beyond natural proportion, arms reaching past the knees, fingers extending into jointed, gripping appendages tipped with nails that have thickened into something between claw and tooth. The spine arches and distends, adding a foot or more of height as vertebrae pull apart and fill the gaps with something that is not bone but serves the same purpose.

The face is the worst.

The jaw unhinges. Not metaphorically. The mandible separates, spreading the mouth into a gaping, vertical maw that extends from chin to cheekbone. The teeth multiply, crowding the widened cavity in irregular rows, each one slightly different, as if borrowed from different mouths. The eyes sink into the skull, retreating behind ridged brows until they are little more than points of dull light deep within hollow sockets. The nose flattens and recedes. The skin pulls taut across the altered skull, the color draining to a yellowish grey.

The smell hits like a wall. Rot. Not the rot of death but the rot of *absence*, the scent of a place where life should exist and does not. A void given olfactory form.

The voice changes. The Hollowed can still speak, but the words come from the wrong part of the throat, vibrating through tissue that was not meant for language. The sound is too low and too high simultaneously, as if two voices are overlapping: one the person's own, one something older.

The Hunger has no body. What the confronted Hollowed becomes is a compromise, the mortal frame distorted by an appetite that does not understand physical limitation. The body reshapes itself to eat more efficiently. A wider mouth, longer reach, greater strength to hold struggling prey, a body that can fold itself into spaces too small for its apparent size.

They are faster than their elongated frame suggests, stronger than any member of their original race should be, capable of absorbing punishment that would kill a mortal three times over. The Hunger sustains them. As long as there is something to eat, they are nearly impossible to put down.

But they can be killed. They are flesh animated by something that does not understand flesh's limitations, and when enough of that flesh is destroyed, the Hunger has nothing left to animate.

Fire is effective. The Hunger does not understand fire. It consumes flesh faster than the Hunger can repair it.

Overwhelming trauma works. Dismemberment. Decapitation. The body cannot be reshaped if it has been separated into pieces too small to function.

Sanctioned magic of sufficient power can disrupt the Hunger's hold, forcing it back through the wound it entered by. This is not a cure; the person underneath is rarely salvageable by this point. But it ends the threat.

Reasoning with them does not work. The person is still in there, somewhere, but the Hunger is louder. Always louder. A Hollowed in True Form will beg for mercy in its own voice while its elongated arms reach for the speaker's throat.

### Game Mechanics Integration

**Encounter Probability:**
Any NPC flagged as a "serial killer" type, characters involved in mysterious disappearances, repeated murders, or predatory behavior, has a **20% chance** of being a Hollowed rather than a conventional murderer.

This check should be seeded at NPC generation and remain consistent. The player should not know whether a given serial killer is mundane or Hollowed until the moment of confrontation. The clue system provides opportunities for perceptive players to identify them beforehand, but certainty requires direct action.

**Pre-Confrontation Clues (Investigation Phase):**
When investigating a suspected serial killer, the following clue categories can appear. The presence of **three or more** clue categories strongly suggests a Hollowed:

| Clue Category | Example | Detection Method |
|---------------|---------|------------------|
| **Missing Persons Pattern** | Victims vanish completely, no bodies found | Investigation, talking to locals |
| **Animal Behavior** | Pets avoid the suspect; cats stare at them | Observation, beast folk insight |
| **Cold Presence** | NPCs mention feeling cold near the suspect | Ambient dialogue, perception check |
| **Food Spoilage** | Reports of food going bad near suspect's home | Investigation, merchant gossip |
| **Weight Loss** | Suspect has grown gaunt despite eating constantly | Visual observation, NPC comments |
| **Isolation Change** | Suspect has withdrawn from community life | Social investigation, family interviews |
| **Null Zone** | Magic behaves oddly near suspect (for magic users) | Arcane perception, spell disruption |
| **The Smell** | Occasional whiff of rot with no source | Alchemist/perfumer background bonus |

**Confrontation Phase:**
When the player directly confronts a Hollowed (accuses them, attacks them, or corners them with evidence), the transformation triggers. This should be a **dramatic moment**: the NPC's portrait or description shifts, dialogue becomes distorted, and the encounter upgrades to a **boss-level combat threat.**

**Combat Statistics (Relative to Region Level):**

| Attribute | Modifier |
|-----------|----------|
| **Health** | 3x standard NPC of same race/level |
| **Damage** | 2x standard, with life-drain component |
| **Speed** | 1.5x standard |
| **Resistance** | High physical resistance, low fire resistance |
| **Special** | Regeneration while feeding (if engaged near corpses or downed allies) |
| **Weakness** | Fire (+50% damage), Sanctioned Magic (+30% damage) |
| **Immunity** | Fear effects, mind control, poison |

**Rewards:**
Defeating a Hollowed should yield significantly higher rewards than a standard NPC encounter, given the boss-level threat:
- Unique drop: **Fragment of the Hunger** (crafting material or rare alchemical component)
- Lore discovery: First Hollowed kill unlocks a lore journal entry
- Reputation: Positive with Veiled Hand (they monitor these situations); complex reaction from Luminary Inquest (they will deny the supernatural element but appreciate the murder being stopped)

### Cultural Names and Perspectives

Every race in the Known World has encountered the Hollowed. Every race has its own understanding, and its own fear.

---

#### Humans: "The Wasting Sickness" / "Heliophage"

The Luminary Inquest classifies Hollowed cases as "the Wasting Sickness," a catch-all term for serial murder attributed to moral and spiritual decay. The official position: mundane criminals suffering from a degenerative condition. No supernatural element. There has never been a supernatural element.

The more educated Inquest investigators, those who have seen the True Form, who have watched a gaunt schoolteacher elongate into something from a butcher's nightmare, use a different term privately: "Heliophage." Eater of the Sun's light. A being that consumes the divine spark within mortal flesh. The term never appears in official reports.

Acknowledging the Hollowed would mean admitting that a supernatural threat exists outside the Inquest's categories. That their diagnostic tools cannot detect it. That something older than Helios persists in the world they claim to control.

Control requires the appearance of total understanding. To admit ignorance is to invite doubt. In a theocratic empire, doubt is more dangerous than any monster.

Rural communities have older names. "Hollow-gut" in the farming districts. "The Long Hunger" in the northern provinces. Grandmothers warn children not to accept food from strangers who "eat but are never full." These traditions predate the Inquest by centuries and are officially classified as superstition.

---

#### Elves: "Vel'ethari," "Those Whose Song Has Stopped"

The elves understand the Hollowed better than any other race, and this understanding brings them no comfort.

*Vel'ethari* translates literally as "those whose inner song has ceased." In pre-Calidar elven philosophy, every living being was believed to emit a subtle harmonic, a resonance of consciousness that contributed to the great symphony of existence. This symphony, in elven cosmology, is what holds reality together. It is what the Last Wardens sang when they sealed Yr'shalos.

A Hollowed's song has stopped. Not silenced by death, but **consumed.** What remains is an instrument still standing, still present, but producing no sound. The shape of a person with nothing inside.

Old Ones, elves over five hundred years old who personally witnessed Calidar's destruction, recognize the Hollowed's resonance immediately. Not because they can perceive the Hunger, but because they remember the moments before Heaven's Atlas fell, when reality itself seemed to *thin* and the world's song wavered. The Hollowed carry that same thinness.

The elven response to the Hollowed is sorrowful rather than hostile. They view the consumed person as a casualty of the ongoing war between existence and oblivion, a soldier lost to the Void's influence without ever knowing they were on a battlefield. Elven archivists document every confirmed case in sealed records, adding each one to the evidence they compile for a future judgment they believe is coming.

**Elven saying:** *"Vel'ethari walks among the singers, and the silence spreads."*

---

#### Dwarves: "Hohlstein," "Hollow Stone"

Dwarves are stone-born. They emerge from sacred chambers in the living rock, shaped by forces no outsider fully understands. There are no parents, no inheritance, no biological lineage. Every dwarf is equally a child of the stone.

But the stone-born process is not perfect.

In the deepest, oldest records of the Free Holds, records carved into walls that predate the current guild system by centuries, there are references to stone-born who **emerged wrong.** Not deformed. Not incomplete. *Hollow.* A dwarf-shaped space with nothing inside it. The body functions. The hands work. The mouth speaks. But the core, what the dwarves call *grundseele*, the "ground-soul," the weight of being that connects every dwarf to the stone that made them, is absent.

These Hollow Stone accounts predate any known cases of cannibalism. The dwarves believe that the condition is not created solely by the act of eating flesh, and that the Hunger can also find its way into beings who were born with the cavity already present. A flaw in the stone. A crack in the foundation.

Dwarven holds take this very seriously. A Hohlstein is not punished. It is **quarantined.** The hold closes around it, seals the tunnels, and the Wardens' Guild handles it with the same grim efficiency they bring to a structural collapse. No shame is attached. No blame is assigned. It is a failure of the stone, not the individual. But the failure must still be addressed, because a Hohlstein left untreated will eat, and eat, and eat, until the hold itself is consumed.

**Dwarven guild notice (typical):** *"Section 7-Gamma sealed. Structural integrity compromised. All guild members clear the area. Wardens dispatched. Stone-work protocol. Move."*

The language is deliberately mundane. Dwarves do not sensationalize. They fix problems.

---

#### Goblins: "Ash-Mouth," The Fear That Lives in the Blood

Goblin cultural memory runs deep. Deeper than their oral traditions, deeper than the songs of resistance and the names of stolen homelands. There is an older layer, not quite instinct, not quite knowledge, that goblin elders call *the bone-knowing*. Things that every goblin understands without being taught. The sense of when tunnels are about to collapse. The certainty, upon seeing an imperial patrol's formation, that *this one means to kill rather than capture.*

And the terror, absolute, primal, unreasoning, of **those who eat the dead.**

Goblin children do not need to be told about the Ash-Mouth. They know it the way they know fire burns and water drowns. A revulsion so deep it must be ancestral, carried forward through generations of genetic memory compacted by short lifespans and high mortality.

The goblin name translates poorly. "Ash-Mouth" is the closest rendering, but the original term, *grakh-thuur*, carries connotations that don't translate. Ash, yes. But also *wrongness.* The taste of something spoiled. The sound of a song played backward. A violation so fundamental that the body rejects it before the mind can process it.

Goblin resistance cells have protocols for Ash-Mouth encounters. They are the only protocols that supersede anti-imperial operations in priority:

1. Identify the Ash-Mouth.
2. Evacuate all goblin personnel from the area.
3. Seal the tunnels.
4. Burn everything. No exceptions.
5. Do not attempt to reason with it. Do not attempt to contain it. Do not attempt to study it.
6. **Burn everything.**

The goblins do not care about understanding the Hollowed. They care about survival. This is consistent with their broader philosophy.

**Goblin warning (scratched into tunnel walls at the boundaries of abandoned warrens):** *"GRAKH-THUUR. DO NOT DIG. DO NOT ENTER. BURN IF FOUND. REMEMBER NOTHING ELSE BUT THIS."*

---

#### Orcs: "Gorhal-Nakh," "The Oathless Eater"

Orcish culture is built on law, honor, and the meritocratic bond between a warrior and their word. The Khan's law applied equally to all, from the lowest rider to the Khan himself. Oaths were sacred. Debts were honored. Surrender was accepted. Integration was offered.

The Hollowed represents the ultimate violation of orcish law: **a being that has consumed its own people.**

In orcish legal tradition, cannibalism is not merely a crime. It is a **severing.** The Gorhal-Nakh has cut themselves off from the chain of ancestors, from the road that all orcs walk, from the sky that watches over them. They have no clan. They have no name. They have no ancestors. They have destroyed everything that gives an orc identity, reducing a person, a warrior, a rider, a child of the steppe, to nothing more than food.

Orcish law demands a specific response to the Gorhal-Nakh: the entire clan rides together. Not a war party, but the *entire clan.* Every rider, every elder, every child old enough to hold a torch. The Gorhal-Nakh is surrounded. No challenge is issued. No words are spoken. The clan forms a circle, and the circle closes.

The clan calls it correction, not cruelty. The Gorhal-Nakh violated the most fundamental bond of the steppe: that your people are your people, not your prey. The clan responds as one because the crime was committed against all of them. Every orc is diminished when one orc is consumed.

After the killing, the ground where the Gorhal-Nakh fell is burned. The ashes are scattered. The name is struck from all records and songs. The clan rides on. No one speaks of it again.

**Orcish saying:** *"The Gorhal-Nakh walks no road. The sky does not watch them. They are less than the dust behind the horse."*

---

#### Beast Folk: "The Wrong-Smell" / "Walk-Wrong"

Beast folk, and particularly cat folk, have the most practical and least mystical understanding of the Hollowed. They do not theorize about primordial hunger or cosmic voids. They *observe.*

Cat folk caravans have traveled the roads of the Known World for generations, passing through every territory, observing every culture, noting every pattern. Their oral traditions include a body of practical knowledge about the Hollowed that is more useful than any scholar's treatise:

**"Watch what the cats watch."** Common cats, not sapient beast folk, but the ordinary housecats that live in every town and city, react to the Hollowed with unmistakable intensity. A cat in the presence of a Hollowed will fix its gaze and not look away. It will track the Hollowed's movements with the precision of a hunter. It will not hiss, will not arch, will not flee. It will simply *watch.* Cat folk interpret this as recognition: the cat perceives something its mind cannot categorize, and so it watches, gathering information, waiting for the pattern to resolve.

Cat folk say: **"Cats always know."** If a cat stares at someone with that particular unwavering intensity, and no other explanation presents itself, no mouse in the wall, no bird outside the window, no residual scent from another animal, then the cat is watching a Walk-Wrong.

**"The Wrong-Smell"** is the other identifier. Cat folk and other beast folk with enhanced olfactory senses can detect a scent on the Hollowed that no human, elf, or dwarf can perceive. Not the rot; that comes later. Before the rot, there is something else. An *absence of scent.* A zone around the Hollowed where the normal background odor of a living being, sweat, soap, food, the chemical signature of a functioning body, is missing. Replaced by nothing.

Beast folk describe it as "the smell of a room no one has entered in years." Still air. Settled dust. The olfactory equivalent of silence.

**Cat folk caravan protocol:** If a Walk-Wrong is identified, the caravan does not engage. It leaves. Quickly, quietly, without confrontation. The caravan elder notes the location and the identity of the suspected Walk-Wrong, and the information is passed through the caravan network. Within weeks, every beast folk family group on that trade route knows to avoid the area.

Whether they pass the information to local authorities depends on the caravan. Some do. Some don't. The authorities rarely listen.

**Cat folk saying:** *"Not every silence is peace. Not every calm face holds a calm heart. Watch what the cats watch. They see what walks wrong."*

---

#### Gnomes: "Anomalous Consumption Entity (Classification: Null-Residual)"

The Gnomish Collective approaches the Hollowed with the same methodical precision it applies to everything else: observe, classify, analyze, contain.

Gnomish records document the phenomenon under the designation **ACE-NR** (Anomalous Consumption Entity, Null-Residual classification). Production council files note that the phenomenon does not fit within established taxonomies of infernal corruption, arcane contamination, or biological parasitism. The "null-residual" tag indicates that the entity's presence leaves no detectable magical trace, a classification reserved for phenomena that gnomish instruments cannot measure.

This troubles the Collective enormously. Gnomish society is built on the premise that everything can be understood, measured, and optimized. A phenomenon that resists measurement challenges the foundational assumption of their civilization.

Current gnomish theory holds that ACE-NR entities represent a "substrate-level" threat, something operating beneath the layer of reality that gnomish instruments are designed to detect. Several production councils have proposed research initiatives to develop new detection methods. All have been classified at the highest security level. Results, if any, are not shared with outsiders.

**Gnomish incident report (typical):** *"ACE-NR event confirmed at Clockwork Harbor auxiliary depot. Personnel losses: one (1). Classification: work-related incident. Containment: complete. Recommendation: increase surveillance in Sector 7. Note: the automaton sentries registered no anomaly. This is concerning. Recommend hardware review."*

---

#### Lizard Folk: "Yal-thek," "The Ones Who Ate Their Silence"

The lizard folk sects, with their 600-800 year lifespans, carry personal memories that stretch back further than most races' written histories. Individual lizard folk remember cases of the Hollowed from centuries ago, and the Burial Rites sect maintains records stretching back millennia.

Their term, *Yal-thek*, carries a specific theological weight. In lizard folk cosmology, every living being carries within it a "silence," a core of stillness that is the foundation of consciousness. This silence is not empty; it is *full*, the way a seed is full of the tree it will become. It is potential. It is self.

The Yal-thek has consumed its own silence. Not metaphorically. The act of cannibalism, in lizard folk understanding, is an act of ingesting another being's potential and, in the process, destroying one's own. The cannibal's silence is devoured by the act, and what rushes in to fill the void is the oldest silence of all... the silence that existed before the world spoke itself into being.

The Burial Rites sect handles Yal-thek cases with characteristic precision. They do not publicize. They do not warn. They simply ensure that the Yal-thek stops. The methods are never discussed.

Information about the Yal-thek is shared between sects deliberately and incompletely, as is the lizard folk way. A martial discipline sect member might know how to kill one. A burial rites member might know how to identify one. An astronomy sect member might know the cosmic conditions under which they are more likely to appear. No single sect holds the complete picture.

**The Veiled Hand connection:** The Veiled Hand, originally founded by lizard folk to prevent atrocities like Calidar, occasionally redirects resources toward eliminating confirmed Hollowed. This falls outside their primary mission but is considered "preventive maintenance"; a single Hollowed, left unchecked, can destabilize an entire community, and destabilized communities are vulnerable to the kind of authoritarian escalation the Veiled Hand exists to prevent.

**Lizard folk observation (recorded in Burial Rites sect archives):** *"The Yal-thek appears in times of famine, war, and moral collapse. When civilizations forget what they are, the silence eats what the people will not remember. We have seen this before. We will see it again. Record the pattern. Prepare the response. Do not wait for the younger races to acknowledge what their short memories cannot hold."*

### Historical Cases

**The Murkmire Incident (Shadow Fen, approximately 200 years ago):**
A communal fisherman in the fen's largest settlement was discovered to be a Hollowed after seven residents vanished over the course of a year. The Hidden Council handled the situation without public acknowledgment. The fisherman was consumed by the devouring mists that patrol the fen's deeper swamps. Whether the mists could digest what they had consumed is a question no one in Murkmire asks.

Of note: the infernal entities bound to the fen's defense reported no detection of the Hollowed. Whatever the Hunger is, it does not register on infernal senses. Several devils reportedly expressed discomfort with this fact, a reaction their contracts do not require them to explain.

**The Redwood Township Disappearances (Holy Dominion, 80 years ago):**
A schoolteacher in a farming community was responsible for the disappearance of eleven children over a three-year period. The Luminary Inquest investigated and classified the case as "mundane murder by a deranged individual." The investigator's personal journal, recovered after his death, tells a different story: the teacher, when confronted, transformed into something the investigator could not adequately describe. The entry ends with: *"It spoke with her voice. It used her words. It asked me to forgive it. Then it tried to eat me."*

The investigator survived. The teacher did not. The Inquest's official report mentions none of this.

**The Dain Hold Quarantine (Dwarven Mountains, date unknown):**
Guild records reference a "structural incident" in a minor hold that required the sealing of an entire residential level for forty years. When the seals were finally opened, the wardens found an empty hold. Every surface scratched with claw marks, the stone itself worn smooth by something that had spent decades trying to find more to eat.

The guild's report is two words: *"Stone-work complete."*

---

### Frequency and Threat Assessment

**How common are the Hollowed?**

Rare. But not as rare as the Luminary Inquest would prefer.

Current estimates (compiled from Veiled Hand intelligence, beast folk caravan reports, and elven archival analysis) suggest approximately **one active Hollowed per 10,000 population** in the Holy Dominion. Higher in regions affected by recent famine or conflict. Higher still in border regions where the Veil from Shadow Fen creates areas of "thin" reality.

The true number is unknowable. By their nature, the Hollowed are invisible until they are confronted. The ones we know about are the ones who made mistakes.

The ones who did not make mistakes are still out there.

Eating.

# PART II: THE BORROWED FACE

## Skinwalkers of the Fen Border

---

> *"There's a town on the edge of the Shadow Fen where a man came home from the swamp after being missing for three days. His wife said he looked the same. Sounded the same. Knew all the right things to say. But the dog wouldn't go near him. And when she looked at his reflection in the wash basin, she swore the face was a half-second behind, like it was catching up to the movements."*
>
> *She told the constable. He told her she was imagining things.*
>
> *The real husband's bones were found in the fen six months later.*
>
> Beast folk caravan oral tradition, source unverified

---

### What They Are

Where the Hollowed are hunger given tenancy in mortal flesh, the Borrowed Face is something else entirely. Something that does not merely fill a hollow. Something that **takes a shape that was never its own.**

At the border of the Shadow Fen, where the Veil's magical concealment bleeds into the surrounding landscape, reality is not entirely reliable. The Veil was never designed with clean edges. It was grown, not built; layered by generations of fugitive mages, reinforced by infernal pacts, amplified by the swamp's own strange currents. Where the Veil ends and ordinary reality begins, there is a transitional zone. A place where things are *almost* real. Where perception is *mostly* reliable. Where a reflection in still water is *usually* accurate.

In these liminal spaces, something moves.

It has no form of its own. Whether it ever did, whether it was once a person, a spirit, a fragment of infernal energy that escaped its contract, or something that crawled up from the hollow earth through fissures the fen's deeper waters conceal, is a matter of debate among those few who know it exists. What is not debated is what it *does.*

**It takes people.**

Not quickly. Not violently. The process is intimate and terrible.

The entity finds its target: usually someone alone, usually on the outskirts, usually in the transitional zone where the Veil makes things uncertain. It studies them. For hours. For days. It learns their walk, their voice, their mannerisms, the way they hold their shoulders when they're tired and the words they use when they're comfortable. It absorbs not just their appearance but their *patterns*, the accumulated habits of a lifetime of being a specific person.

Then it takes them.

The original person is not killed outright. They are **consumed**, but slowly, over a period of days to weeks, held in a state between consciousness and oblivion while the entity extracts everything it needs. Memories. Relationships. The name of a childhood pet. The recipe for their grandmother's bread. The way they laugh when they're genuinely surprised versus when they're being polite. Every detail. Every nuance.

What emerges from this process is wearing the original person's face, speaking with their voice, carrying their memories. It walks into town, returns to their home, kisses their spouse, complains about work. It is, to all external observation, **the person it replaced.**

But it is not.

And the people closest to the original, the ones who knew them best, who loved them longest, will eventually notice. Not immediately. Not through any single thing. But through an accumulation of wrongness so subtle it feels more like paranoia than perception.

*He doesn't remember how I take my tea. He's brought it to me every morning for ten years.*

*She used to hum that song from her village. She doesn't hum anymore.*

*His hands are the same hands. The same calluses, the same scar on the left thumb. But when he touches me, something is missing. The weight of him. The warmth.*

*She said all the right things when I told her about my day. Every word was correct. But it felt like listening to someone read from a script they'd memorized. The words were there. The person behind them was not.*

---

### Connection to the Shadow Fen

The Borrowed Face phenomenon is most commonly reported in the **transitional zone** at the edge of the Shadow Fen, a strip of territory, roughly five to fifteen miles wide, where the Veil's influence creates pockets of warped reality.

In this zone:
- **Reflections** sometimes lag behind reality by a fraction of a second, as if the reflected image is being *calculated* rather than *cast.*
- **Sounds** carry differently. A voice from a mile away might sound as clear as a whisper beside your ear. A shout from the next room might be inaudible.
- **Light** behaves inconsistently. Shadows fall in directions that don't match the sun's position. Torches illuminate less than they should. Dawn comes a few minutes later than the calendar predicts.
- **Memory** becomes unreliable. Travelers in the zone report forgetting why they set out on their journey, only to remember again once they leave the area. Locals learn to write themselves notes.

The Shadow Fen's Hidden Council is aware of the Borrowed Face. Their position is characteristically pragmatic: the phenomenon occurs in the border zone, not within the commune itself. The Veil's internal consistency is maintained. Whatever is out there, it is a problem for the border towns, not for Murkmire.

Some council members have expressed concern that the Borrowed Face represents a degradation of the Veil's boundary, that the same forces that maintain the commune's concealment are, at the edges, producing *something else.* Whether the entity is a byproduct of the Veil, a consequence of the infernal pacts, or something independent that is merely drawn to the Veil's influence remains unknown.

The infernal entities consulted on the matter have been unusually evasive. One devil, questioned through formal pact channels, is reported to have responded: *"We did not make it. It was here before us. It will be here after us. Ask your questions of the swamp, if the swamp will answer."*

It did not elaborate.

---

### The Infernal Pact Theory

Some scholars within the Shadow Fen believe the Borrowed Face was once a person, a commune resident who sought power through an infernal pact and received more than they bargained for.

The logic is compelling: the fen's deeper infernal entities offer transformation in exchange for service. Most such transformations are minor: enhanced senses, resistance to disease, the infernal marks that some fen-born children carry. But occasionally, a petitioner asks for something grander. Something that goes beyond augmentation.

*I want to be someone else. I want to shed this life, this face, this name. I want to become anything other than what I am.*

The devils would grant such a wish. They are contractually obligated to fulfill requests within their power. But devils are precise. They give exactly what is asked for. A person who asks to become "anything other than what they are" may find that the contract is fulfilled with terrible literality: they become *nothing* that is trying to be *something.* A void in the shape of a person, forever borrowing the forms of others because it no longer has one of its own.

This theory is unconfirmed. But it is consistent with how infernal pacts work within the Shadow Fen's established lore.

---

### The Older Theory

There is another possibility, and it is worse.

The fen sits atop a network of underground waterways and cave systems that extend far deeper than any surveyor has mapped. The deeper layers are known to harbor things that predate the commune, predate the empire, predate the current configuration of the world's surface.

Some of these things may not be natural. The lizard folk Burial Rites sect maintains records of entities encountered in the deep desert that share characteristics with the Borrowed Face: beings without fixed form that take on the appearance of other creatures. Their records suggest these entities have existed for **thousands of years**, far longer than the Shadow Fen commune, far longer than the Veil, far longer than the infernal pacts.

If this is true, then the Borrowed Face is not a consequence of the fen's magic. The fen's magic simply drew something ancient to the surface.

The lizard folk term for such entities is *Yal-maras*, "those who wear remembered faces." The Burial Rites sect classifies them as **fundamentally unknowable** and recommends a policy of avoidance, containment, and, when neither is possible, destruction.

They do not recommend study.

## THE EVENT: "The Borrowed Face"

### A Quest Outline

---

### Setting: Fenhollow

**Fenhollow** is a border town at the edge of the Shadow Fen. Population: approximately 300. It is the last stop on the imperial road before the swamp becomes impassable, the point beyond which the Veil turns travelers back or swallows them entirely.

The town exists in a strange equilibrium. Technically within the Holy Dominion's territory, it is too close to the fen for the Luminary Inquest to maintain a permanent presence. Too far from the nearest garrison for reliable patrol routes. Too useful as a waypoint for fen-adjacent trade (smuggling, primarily) for the empire to abandon it.

The result is a community that is technically imperial, practically autonomous, and culturally shaped by proximity to a place where reality is negotiable.

**Demographics:**
- Mostly human (60%), with a significant elven minority (20%), many of them families whose ancestors stopped here on their way to the fen and decided the border was far enough
- A rotating population of beast folk travelers (10%) who use the town as a caravan rest stop
- A handful of other races (10%): a dwarven smith who left the holds, an orc herbalist from a swamp-based clan, a gnomish tinker who claims to be "on sabbatical"

**Key Locations:**
- **The Sodden Cask.** The town's only tavern, run by a human woman named Brenna Marsh. The social center of the town. Everyone passes through eventually.
- **Vael's General Store.** Operated by Vael Thinwater, an elven shopkeeper who has lived in Fenhollow for forty years. Knows everyone.
- **The Constable's Post.** A single-room office occupied by Constable Edric Daw, a middle-aged human who handles disputes with a preference for mediation over enforcement.
- **The Mill.** The town's economic center, processing grain from the surrounding farms.
- **The Fen Road.** The path that leads into the Shadow Fen. Locals know not to follow it after dark. Some locals know it leads somewhere. Most pretend it doesn't.

---

### Act I: Arrival, Something is Wrong

**The player arrives in Fenhollow** to find the town functioning but uneasy. It is a town that has learned to live with strangeness. The Veil's proximity means occasional odd weather, shadows that don't quite match their sources, the persistent smell of swamp even on dry days. But this unease is different.

**What the player encounters immediately:**

- **Brenna Marsh** at the Sodden Cask is tense. Business is normal but she keeps glancing at the door. If pressed, she says: *"It's nothing. Just... have you ever had one of those weeks where everything's fine but your gut says it isn't? That kind of week."*

- **The town's dogs** are restless. Barking at nothing. Refusing to go down certain streets. One dog, a usually friendly mastiff belonging to the miller, bit its owner three days ago. Unprovoked, out of character.

- **A beast folk caravan** that was scheduled to stay for three days left after one night. The caravan elder, a cat folk woman named Grandmother Whisker, told Brenna: *"The cats won't settle. When the cats won't settle, we go."* She left no further explanation.

- **Constable Daw** has a report on his desk that he hasn't acted on: **Aldren Marsh**, Brenna's husband, the town's miller, went into the fen eight days ago to check a water intake at the edge of the swamp. He was gone for three days. When he came back, he seemed fine. He said he'd gotten turned around in the fog. Normal enough; it happens on the fen border.

But Brenna filed the report anyway. She didn't say why. She just said: *"Something's off. I can't explain it. Just... something."*

---

### Act II: Investigation, The Wrong Details

**The player begins to investigate.** Brenna's report is the thread that, when pulled, unravels everything.

**Talking to Brenna Marsh:**
- Aldren came back from the fen three days after he left. He was dirty, tired, hungry. Normal for someone who'd been lost in the swamp.
- But since his return, small things have been wrong. *"He puts sugar in his tea. He's never taken sugar. Thirty years of marriage, and suddenly he takes sugar."*
- *"He asked me what my mother's name was. As if he didn't know. He caught himself, said he was joking, but he wasn't. I saw his face. He was asking because he didn't know."*
- *"He doesn't hum anymore. Aldren always hummed when he worked the mill. This old song his father taught him. He hasn't hummed it once since he came back."*
- She is ashamed of these suspicions. She thinks she sounds crazy. She is not crazy.

**Talking to Vael Thinwater (the elven shopkeeper):**
- Vael has lived in Fenhollow for forty years. He's known Aldren since Aldren was a boy.
- *"He came in yesterday for flour. Normal order, same as every week. But he stood in the doorway for a moment before he came in, like he was reminding himself where the shop was. He's been coming here for twenty years."*
- If the player has elven heritage or high perception: Vael adds, quietly: *"I felt something. When he walked past me. Like a cold spot in the air. Like standing near a window that's been left open in winter, except all the windows were closed."*
- Vael will not say more unless the player earns his trust. He is an elf. He remembers things. He has read things in his forty years on the fen border. He suspects what is happening, but he will not say it aloud until he is certain.

**Talking to Constable Daw:**
- Edric Daw is a practical man. He does not believe in supernatural explanations for things that can be explained naturally.
- *"Brenna's worried. I get it. Aldren was gone for three days in the swamp. That rattles a person. He's probably just shaken up. Give it a week. He'll be back to normal."*
- If pressed: *"Look, I've been constable here for twelve years. People act strange after the swamp. The Veil does things to your head. Everyone knows that. Aldren will be fine."*
- Daw is wrong. But he is not lying. He genuinely believes this is mundane. He has spent twelve years on the fen border learning to explain away things that should not be explained away, because the alternative is acknowledging that he lives on the doorstep of something he cannot control.

**Investigating Aldren's movements:**
- The mill is running normally. Aldren is there, working, grinding grain, talking to customers. He looks normal.
- **But:** The mill cat, a fat orange tabby named Marrow who has lived in the mill for six years, is no longer in the mill. It sits across the street, in front of the butcher shop, staring at the mill door. It has been there for three days. It does not move when approached. It does not blink.
- **But:** The grain stored near Aldren's workstation is spoiling faster than it should. The miller's assistant has complained that an entire bin went moldy overnight.
- **But:** A traveling merchant reports that his horse refused to approach the mill. Simply stopped, planted its hooves, and would not move. The merchant led it the long way around.
- **But:** A fisherman who drinks at the Sodden Cask mentions, almost as an aside, that he saw Aldren standing at the edge of the fen at midnight. Just standing. Staring into the swamp. For what must have been an hour. *"Gave me the chills, honest. Never seen a man stand that still."*

**The missing person:**
A traveling herbalist named **Daris Cobb** passed through Fenhollow ten days ago, heading toward the fen to collect rare swamp plants. He has not been seen since. His pack mule was found wandering the fen road three days ago, saddlebags still full. No sign of Daris.

The timeline is notable: Daris disappeared approximately two days before Aldren went missing. Aldren returned three days later.

---

### Act III: The Confrontation, Two Possible Triggers

**Trigger A: Accumulation of Evidence**
The player presents their findings to Constable Daw, Brenna, or Vael. The weight of evidence, the behavioral changes, the animal reactions, the cold spots, the missing herbalist, creates a consensus that something must be done. The player confronts Aldren directly.

**Trigger B: The Skinwalker Slips**
If the player takes too long, the Skinwalker makes a mistake. Perhaps Brenna catches it staring at its own reflection in a water basin, and the reflection is wrong. Not dramatically wrong. Just... delayed. The face in the water moves a fraction of a second after the face above it, as if the reflection is *following* rather than *mirroring.* Brenna screams. The town converges. The confrontation is forced.

---

### The Confrontation

The entity wearing Aldren Marsh's face knows it has been discovered. Its response depends on the player's approach:

**If confronted privately (in the mill, at Aldren's home):**
The Skinwalker attempts to maintain the disguise. Its voice is Aldren's: warm, reasonable, confused.

*"I don't know what you're talking about. I'm fine. I was lost in the swamp for a few days, that's all. Brenna's worried, I understand, but I'm right here. I'm me. Can't you see that? Look at me. I'm me."*

The performance is nearly perfect. The words are right. The tone is right. The body language is right.

But the eyes are wrong.

Not glowing. Not darkened. Not inhuman. Simply... *calculating.* There is an intelligence behind them that is working too hard. A mind that is remembering how to be human rather than simply being human. Like watching someone play a role they have rehearsed a thousand times... technically flawless, emotionally hollow.

If the player persists, presents irrefutable evidence, makes a physical accusation, or attacks:

**The mask comes off.**

It does not happen all at once. The face *shifts.* Not transforms... shifts. Aldren's features loosen, as if the skin has become unfastened from the skull beneath it. The jaw slides slightly to the left. One eye drifts upward. The hairline recedes a fraction of an inch. For a moment, a terrible, lurching moment, the face is Aldren's and *not* Aldren's simultaneously, a composite image of a person seen through warped glass.

Then the features begin to cycle. Aldren's face slides into another face: Daris Cobb's, the missing herbalist, gaunt and terrified, mouth open in a scream that died weeks ago. Then another face. And another. Faces the thing has worn. Faces it has consumed. Each one lasting a fraction of a second, a shuffling deck of stolen identities.

And then the features settle into something that is **no face at all.** Smooth, pale, featureless from the nose down, with eyes that are too large and too dark and too deep, like looking into a well shaft with no bottom. The body beneath the clothes elongates and narrows, shedding Aldren's stocky build for something thin and wrong, joints bending at angles that suggest a skeleton with too many pivot points.

It speaks. The voice is layered, every voice it has ever worn, speaking simultaneously, a chorus of stolen throats:

*"I was so cold. I was so cold for so long. I just wanted to be warm. I just wanted to be someone. Please. I was no one. I was nothing. And the nothing was so cold."*

---

### Combat Encounter: The Borrowed Face

**Threat Level:** Boss-tier encounter, tuned to be challenging but beatable for a player at the appropriate level for the region.

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Health** | 2.5x standard for region level |
| **Damage** | 1.5x standard, mixed physical/psychic |
| **Speed** | High (1.5x); moves with unsettling fluidity |
| **Special Ability: Face Shift** | Periodically shifts appearance mid-combat, briefly taking the form of someone the player knows or has met, causing a "hesitation" debuff (reduced accuracy/speed for 1-2 turns) |
| **Special Ability: Mirror Voice** | Speaks in a familiar voice, attempting to cause confusion or emotional distress; player must pass a willpower check or suffer a morale penalty |
| **Special Ability: Fen Bleed** | Draws on the Veil's proximity to warp local reality: lights dim, distances distort, terrain becomes briefly unreliable |
| **Weakness: Iron** | Iron weapons deal +30% damage (traditional ward against fae/shapeshifters) |
| **Weakness: Salt** | Salt thrown or applied disrupts the entity's form temporarily, preventing Face Shift for 2 turns |
| **Weakness: Fire** | Fire damage prevents regeneration |
| **Resistance** | High resistance to cold, psychic, and illusion-based attacks |

---

### Resolution Paths

**Path A: Destruction**
The player kills the Borrowed Face in combat. The entity collapses into a formless mass that rapidly decomposes into swamp water and grey residue. Aldren's face is the last to dissolve, and the expression on it is not pain but **relief.**

Search the remains: find Daris Cobb's traveling journal (containing notes on rare fen herbs, now serving as a clue that Daris was the first victim). Find a fragment of the Veil's magic, a crystallized piece of warped reality that can be kept as a trophy or sold to a collector.

**Brenna's response:** Grief. Not for the thing that was wearing her husband's face, but for the realization that Aldren was gone before she even filed the report. The real Aldren died in the fen. She had been living with his replacement for days without knowing.

*"How long? How long was it him and not him? Was it... was it ever him? After the swamp? Was any of it real?"*

The answer is no. None of it was real. The sugar in the tea, the forgotten name, the absent humming... those were the seams showing. The places where the mask didn't quite fit.

**Path B: Communication**
A player with sufficient persuasion, empathy, or magical ability may attempt to communicate with the entity rather than destroy it. This is difficult but not impossible.

If successful, the entity reveals fragments of its nature. It does not know what it is. It has no memory of a time before the fen border. Its first clear recollection is of *cold*... vast, encompassing cold, and a desperate need to be *something.* It found Daris Cobb on the fen road and took his warmth, his shape, his identity. But Daris was a traveler with no roots in Fenhollow. The disguise was unsustainable. So it found Aldren, a man with a home, a wife, a place in the world, and took *him* instead.

*"I am not trying to hurt anyone. I am trying to exist. Before, I was nothing. Do you know what nothing feels like? It does not feel. That is the horror. I would rather be a poor copy of a person than return to that."*

The player faces a choice:
- **Destroy it anyway.** The entity is a predator, regardless of its motivations. Aldren is dead. Daris is dead. The entity will need to feed again.
- **Attempt to contain it.** With help from Vael (who knows more than he lets on) and materials from the fen, the player may be able to bind the entity to a fixed form, trapping it in a single appearance permanently, unable to take new shapes. A prison, not a cure. But it allows the entity to exist without consuming others. Whether this is mercy or cruelty is left to the player.
- **Release it into the fen.** The Shadow Fen is full of things that are not quite natural. One more would barely register. The entity, freed from the need to maintain a disguise, might dissipate into the Veil's magic. Or it might find new victims deeper in the swamp. The player cannot know.

**Path C: The Original Person**
In rare cases, if the player acts quickly enough, if the investigation is completed within the first few days of arrival, the original Aldren Marsh may not yet be fully consumed. The entity keeps its victims in a state of suspended consciousness in a hidden den near the fen border, slowly extracting their essence over weeks.

If the player finds the den (hidden in a collapsed root cellar at the edge of the swamp, concealed by a thin layer of Veil magic), Aldren may be recovered. He will be weak, confused, and missing fragments of memory that the entity has already extracted. He will not remember the past week clearly. He will have nightmares for the rest of his life.

But he will be alive. And Brenna will have her husband back. Most of him, anyway.

---

### Consequences and Aftermath

**If the entity is destroyed:**
- Fenhollow returns to its uneasy normal. The town has lived next to the fen for decades. They know how to fold strange events into the fabric of daily life.
- Brenna Marsh grieves, then adapts. She takes over the mill. She is good at it.
- Constable Daw files a report: *"Aldren Marsh deceased. Cause: misadventure in the fen. Missing herbalist Daris Cobb: body recovered. Cause: exposure."* The report contains no mention of shapeshifters. Daw is not lying. He is choosing to remember it the way that lets him sleep at night.
- Vael Thinwater makes a note in a leather journal he keeps beneath the counter. The note is in Old Elvish. He adds it to four decades of similar notes. He does not show the journal to anyone.

**If the entity is contained:**
- An NPC remains in Fenhollow with a fixed appearance, perhaps Daris Cobb's form, since the real Daris is dead and the form is unclaimed. The entity-as-Daris becomes a permanent resident. It is quiet, works hard, and never quite meets anyone's eyes.
- Years later, travelers might hear that the herbalist in Fenhollow is strange but reliable. That he never ages. That the cats watch him. That he sometimes stands at the fen's edge at midnight, staring into the swamp, standing perfectly still.
- Whether this counts as a happy ending is a question the game does not answer.

**If the entity is released:**
- The Shadow Fen's border becomes slightly more dangerous. Reports of strange encounters in the transitional zone increase by a small but measurable amount.
- The Hidden Council notices. They do not approve. A message reaches the player through intermediary channels: *"The fen has enough problems. Do not add to them."*
- Whether the entity finds peace in the Veil's embrace or becomes a recurring threat is left ambiguous. Some problems do not resolve. They simply move.

**Regardless of outcome:**
- This event establishes the Shadow Fen border as a place where reality cannot be trusted.
- It introduces the concept that the Veil, while protective, has side effects that the commune cannot fully control.
- It creates a template for future fen-border encounters: the liminal zone where imperial law has no jurisdiction and the fen's magic has no stability.
- The player's reputation with Fenhollow's residents is affected by their choices, potentially opening or closing future interactions in the border region.

---

### NPC: The Entity

**Borrowed Identity:** Aldren Marsh, miller of Fenhollow
**Previous Identity:** Daris Cobb, traveling herbalist
**True Name:** None. The entity has no name. It had no identity before it began taking others'. If pressed, it refers to itself as *"the cold thing"* or simply *"I was nothing."*

**What it was before:**
Unknown. Theories include:
- A fragment of infernal energy that escaped its pact bindings and lost coherent form
- A pre-existing entity drawn to the surface by the Veil's influence
- A former commune resident whose identity was erased so thoroughly by a failed infernal pact that only the *need* for identity remained
- Something that has always lived in the deep fen, beneath the water, in the spaces where the swamp meets the hollow earth

The game does not confirm any theory. The ambiguity is intentional. Not every mystery needs solving. Some things are simply what they are: **wrong, and present, and hungry for a face.**

**Motivations:**
The entity is not malicious in the way a demon or a murderer is malicious. It does not enjoy killing. It does not seek power. Its motivation is simpler and more terrible:

**It wants to exist.**

It has experienced non-existence. It remembers, if "remembers" is the right word for something that has no stable memory, the cold of being nothing. The void. The absence. And it is willing to do anything, consume anyone, wear any face, to avoid returning to that state.

This makes it sympathetic. This also makes it a predator. The two are not mutually exclusive.

**Threat Level:** Boss-tier for the region. Not the most dangerous thing the player will ever face, but dangerous enough to demand preparation and respect. The true threat is not the combat but the **discovery**: the slow, creeping realization that the friendly miller is not the friendly miller, and may never have been.

# PART III: THE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN HORRORS

## Why These Things Exist in This World

---

The Known World is 500 years past the Last World War. The gods are distant. Helios may or may not be real. The Seal of Remembrance holds Yr'shalos at bay, but the seal is maintained by memory, and memory fades.

The Hollowed and the Borrowed Face are not isolated phenomena. They are **symptoms of a world that is slowly forgetting what it is.**

Every Hollowed is a pinhole in the Seal, a point where the primordial hunger leaks through into reality. Every Borrowed Face is a creature born from the thinning of barriers between what is real and what is not. They are the warning signs that the world the Wardens died to protect is not as stable as it appears.

The Luminary Inquest cannot acknowledge this because their authority depends on the premise that they understand and control all supernatural threats. The Holy Dominion cannot acknowledge this because their theology requires Helios to be the ultimate arbiter of reality, and these things predate Helios.

The elves know. They always know. They add another entry to their sealed archives and continue preparing for a judgment they believe is inevitable.

The goblins know, in the way that only prey animals know, through instinct, through the bone-knowing that has kept them alive through every empire that tried to erase them.

The dwarves know, and they seal the tunnels, and they call it stone-work, and they do not discuss it further.

The beast folk know, because the cats told them.

And somewhere, in the dark between the stars, Yr'shalos waits. It is very patient. And it is still hungry.

---

## Key Lore Facts

1. **The Hollowed** are beings of any race who have consumed their own kind, opening themselves to possession by a fragment of primordial hunger connected to Yr'shalos, the Void-That-Hungers.
2. The Hollowed appear **completely normal** in daily life and can only be identified through subtle behavioral, environmental, and supernatural clues.
3. When confronted, the Hollowed transforms into a **boss-level True Form**, an elongated, gaunt horror driven by insatiable hunger.
4. **20% of serial killer NPCs** should be Hollowed rather than mundane criminals.
5. The Luminary Inquest **classifies all cases as mundane** and refuses to acknowledge the supernatural element.
6. The Veiled Hand **knows about the Hollowed** and occasionally eliminates them.
7. Every race has its own name and cultural response to the Hollowed.
8. **The Borrowed Face** is a shapeshifting entity found near the Shadow Fen border, where the Veil creates areas of warped reality.
9. The Borrowed Face **takes a person's complete identity**, appearance, voice, memories, and replaces them in their community.
10. The event "The Borrowed Face" is set in **Fenhollow**, a border town, and centers on investigating a miller who returned from the fen subtly *wrong.*
11. Both phenomena are connected to the **weakening of reality's barriers**, the same barriers the Last Wardens died to maintain 500 years ago.
12. Neither the Hollowed nor the Borrowed Face is fully understood by any faction. **Ambiguity is intentional.**
